Top 5 U.S. Dividend ETFs vs Treasury Bonds

A practical comparison of SCHD, VYM, VIG, DGRO, and SDY using strategy, pros and cons, dividend style, and 10-year total return against a Treasury bond ETF proxy.

Dividend ETFs are popular because they offer investors a simple way to own a diversified basket of dividend-paying companies. Instead of buying individual dividend stocks one by one, investors can use one ETF to gain exposure to dozens or even hundreds of companies.

In this guide, we compare five traditional U.S. dividend ETFs: SCHD, VYM, VIG, DGRO, and SDY. We also compare their long-term total return against IEF, an ETF that tracks intermediate-term U.S. Treasury bonds.

Important note: This is not a prediction. The return comparison below is based on historical total return data with dividends reinvested. Future returns may be very different.

1. SCHD: Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF

SCHD is one of the most popular dividend ETFs among long-term investors. It focuses on U.S. companies with dividend history, quality characteristics, and attractive valuation metrics.

Pros

  • Strong balance between dividend yield and dividend growth
  • Low expense ratio
  • Quality-focused stock selection
  • Popular with long-term income investors

Cons

  • More concentrated than broader dividend ETFs
  • Can underperform growth-heavy markets
  • Sector exposure can shift depending on index methodology
Best for: Investors who want a balanced dividend ETF with quality, yield, and long-term growth potential.

2. VYM: Vanguard High Dividend Yield ETF

VYM is a broad, diversified high-dividend ETF from Vanguard. It tends to hold a large number of companies and is often used by investors who want simple exposure to higher-yielding U.S. stocks.

Pros

  • Very broad diversification
  • Low expense ratio
  • Simple high-dividend strategy
  • Backed by Vanguard’s low-cost fund structure

Cons

  • Less selective than quality-focused dividend ETFs
  • May include slower-growth companies
  • Dividend yield alone does not guarantee superior total return
Best for: Investors who want broad U.S. dividend exposure with low cost and high diversification.

3. VIG: Vanguard Dividend Appreciation ETF

VIG focuses on companies with a history of increasing dividends. It is not necessarily designed to provide the highest current yield. Instead, it emphasizes dividend growth and quality.

Pros

  • Strong dividend growth focus
  • Low expense ratio
  • Quality tilt toward companies with durable earnings
  • Can perform well in long-term compounding strategies

Cons

  • Lower current yield than some high-dividend ETFs
  • May feel less attractive for investors seeking immediate income
  • Can overlap with broad market index funds
Best for: Investors who care more about dividend growth and total return than maximum current yield.

4. DGRO: iShares Core Dividend Growth ETF

DGRO is another major dividend growth ETF. It focuses on companies with sustainable dividend growth and avoids the highest-yielding names that may represent dividend traps.

Pros

  • Good balance between dividend growth and diversification
  • Low cost
  • Broad portfolio of dividend-growing companies
  • Designed to avoid unsustainable payout profiles

Cons

  • Lower current yield than SCHD or VYM
  • Not ideal for investors who need high immediate income
  • Can behave similarly to other dividend growth strategies
Best for: Investors who want diversified dividend growth with a focus on sustainability.

5. SDY: SPDR S&P Dividend ETF

SDY focuses on companies with long dividend growth histories. It is often associated with a dividend aristocrat-style approach and has a longer history than many newer dividend ETFs.

Pros

  • Focus on companies with long dividend track records
  • More traditional dividend strategy
  • Can appeal to conservative income investors
  • Different methodology from SCHD, VYM, VIG, and DGRO

Cons

  • Higher expense ratio than several competitors
  • May lag lower-cost dividend growth ETFs
  • Can be tilted toward slower-growth sectors
Best for: Investors who value long dividend histories and a more traditional dividend aristocrat-style approach.

Dividend ETFs vs Treasury Bonds

Dividend ETFs and Treasury bonds serve different purposes. Dividend ETFs are equity investments. They can grow over time, but they can also decline sharply during bear markets. Treasury bonds are generally safer from a credit perspective, but they are still exposed to interest rate risk.

Category Dividend ETFs Treasury Bonds / IEF Proxy
Growth potential Higher Lower
Income stability Variable dividends Bond interest payments
Price volatility Equity market risk Interest rate risk
Best role Long-term wealth building Portfolio stability and income

Key Takeaways

  • Dividend ETFs are not bond substitutes. They are still stock funds.
  • VIG and DGRO emphasize dividend growth more than high current yield.
  • SCHD offers a popular balance of yield, quality, and value.
  • VYM provides broad high-dividend exposure with low cost.
  • SDY focuses on long dividend history but comes with a higher expense ratio.
  • Treasury bonds may provide stability, but their long-term return can be much lower than equity dividend ETFs.

Final Thoughts

For long-term investors, dividend ETFs can be powerful tools when dividends are reinvested and held through market cycles. However, investors should not focus only on yield.

The best dividend ETF depends on the investor’s objective. SCHD may fit investors seeking a balance of income and quality. VYM may fit investors seeking broad high-yield exposure. VIG and DGRO may fit investors focused on dividend growth. SDY may appeal to those who prefer long dividend histories.

Treasury bonds still have a role in a portfolio, but they serve a different purpose. Dividend ETFs are generally better suited for long-term growth and income, while Treasury bonds are better suited for stability, liquidity, and risk reduction.
